01

The Problem

The Solow Model predicts that economies with higher investment, lower population growth, and lower initial income should grow faster and converge toward the steady state of income per capita.

Do the data support these predictions for LATAM and EAP?

What we test

Investment and Income

Does higher investment relate to higher income per capita?

Income Convergence

Do poorer economies grow faster than richer ones?

Population Growth and Income

Does lower population growth relate to higher income per capita?

02

The Regions

We compare two regions with different development trajectories.

Latin America & Caribbean (LAC)

  • 32 economies with complete data
  • ~ 660 million people
  • Rich in natural resources

East Asia & Pacific (EAP)

  • 31 economies with complete data
  • ~ 2.4 billion people
  • High savings and investment

07

Limitations

Sample size

With 24–32 countries per test, statistical power is limited, especially for H1 in LATAM (n = 24), where missing investment data drops several countries from the regression.

Country size is ignored

Each country counts as one data point regardless of population. EAP spans China (~1.4B people) and Pacific microstates (under 50,000 people each), a 56x income-per-capita range vs. 25x in LATAM. This heterogeneity likely explains why EAP's relationships are weaker and noisier.

Linear correlation only

Pearson's r only captures straight-line relationships. Solow's diminishing returns imply some curvature near the steady state that a linear correlation could underestimate.

Omitted variables & causality

We don't control for human capital, technology (TFP) or institutions, all part of an extended Solow framework. Richer countries can also better afford investment, so causality could run in either direction.

08

Does the Solow Model Hold?

The Solow Model holds across all three predictions in LATAM, but only one of three in EAP. Differences in regional composition (EAP spans a far wider range of income levels and includes both a massive economy (China) and several Pacific microstates) help explain why the relationships are noisier there (see Limitations).
